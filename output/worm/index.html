Worm

A self-replicating malware program that spreads across networks without human intervention.

Description

In cybersecurity, a worm is a type of malicious software (malware) that can replicate itself and spread across computer networks without the need for human action. Unlike viruses, which require a host file to spread, worms exploit vulnerabilities in operating systems or applications to propagate. Once a worm infects a system, it can create copies of itself and send those copies to other systems, often leading to widespread infections. Worms can consume bandwidth, degrade performance, and may also carry payloads that cause further damage to the infected systems or steal sensitive data. Notable characteristics of worms include their ability to operate independently, their rapid spread, and the potential to create backdoors for additional attacks. Famous examples include the Morris Worm from 1988, which was one of the first worms to spread across the internet, and the more recent WannaCry ransomware worm that exploited vulnerabilities in Windows systems to cause significant global disruption.

Examples

  • Morris Worm (1988) - One of the first worms to propagate across the internet.
  • WannaCry (2017) - A ransomware worm that exploited vulnerabilities in Windows to spread rapidly.

Additional Information

  • Worms can spread through various means, including email attachments, network shares, and vulnerabilities in software.
  • Preventative measures include regular software updates, firewalls, and antivirus solutions to detect and block worm activity.
