Work factor

A measure of the amount of effort required to defeat a security mechanism.

Description

In cybersecurity, the term 'work factor' refers to the estimated amount of computational effort or resources needed to break a cryptographic algorithm or to compromise a security system. It quantifies the difficulty an attacker would face in attempting to gain unauthorized access or decrypt information. Work factor takes into account various elements, including the complexity of the algorithm, the length of the cryptographic keys, and the available computational power to the attacker. A higher work factor indicates a more secure system, as it would require significant time, resources, or both to breach. Work factor is crucial for assessing the strength of encryption methodologies, password security, and other protective measures. For example, a work factor that is too low may suggest that a password can be brute-forced easily, while a high work factor may require an impractical amount of time or resources to overcome. It is a key consideration for security professionals when designing systems and deciding on appropriate security measures.

Examples

  • A password hashing algorithm with a work factor of 10 may require one second to compute a hash, while a work factor of 12 may require approximately four seconds.
  • In cryptographic systems, a work factor that takes years to solve indicates a very strong encryption method, making it impractical for attackers.

Additional Information

  • Work factor can be adjusted in certain algorithms to increase security as computational power increases.
  • The concept is also applicable beyond cryptography, including physical security measures and social engineering tactics.
